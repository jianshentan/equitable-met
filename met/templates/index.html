Towards Equitable Met Ticketing
On March 1st The Met changed its 50 year pay-what-you-want admission policy so that out of state visitors must pay a flat $25.


If the Met must increase its admissions revenue, its strategy should prioritize accessibility.

Why This Matters
The Met's value is not universal:
It reflects the priorities and perspectives of its affluent, largely white, founders and donors.
“[Museums] cannot separate themselves from the collective memories that link their development with white supremacy, hetero-patriarchy, abuse of labor, colonization, imperialist theft of art and artifacts, destruction or absencing of alternative ways of interpreting and representing art and artifacts, structural racism and other oppressions.”
MASS Action
For first-time visitors outside typical museum demographics, the cost of entry can go far beyond ticket price:
Unaccounted Cost and Labor

Beyond additional costs–transportation, food, childcare, and time diverted from paid or domestic work– there is the additional labor of investing in a space without guarantee that it is invested in you; navigating cultural codes of a historically white institution; submitting to close surveillance; and viewing one’s culture through a western gaze.
A flat $25 is equal, but disregards the unaccounted cost and labor.
A sliding scale is equitable, taking into account the additional cost and labor.
The Met houses “significant works of art across all times and cultures.” It also pledges to “reach out to the widest possible audience in a spirit of inclusiveness.”

A flat $25 admission fee cuts off the very community whom the Met seeks to serve in its mission, especially marginalized, low-income out-of-state visitors and undocumented individuals.
Where the Met Stands
  
$41.7M

The Met’s single-ticket revenue in 2016-17. The Met says that admissions revenue has grown proportionally slower than its operating costs.
  
$48-53M

The Met’s goal for their new admissions policy is to raise ticket revenue by $6-11 million dollars.
  
$9.13

The average ticket price paid by the Met’s ~4.5M visitors in 2017. The Met’s suggested price for an adult ticket is $25.
